Apt paraphrases

A candidate paraphrase is an apt paraphrase of the original text if the message it conveys is the same or similar to the original text. Please use your intuition to answer the question about the aptness of the candidate paraphrase; there are no right or wrong answers. However, we may ask you to explain your rating if it deviates greatly from the majority.

Literal language

A sentence uses literal language if it conveys meaning directly, describing an object or an event as it is, and using words that are typically associated with the object or event. Literal language constrasts with figurative language, which can take the form of metaphor, personification, simile, idiom, pun, hyperbole, etc.

Please consider the literalness and aptness of a paraphrase as separate criteria. A candidate paraphrase can fully capture the meaning of the original text and thus counts as an apt paraphrase, but does so via the use of non-literal language. Similarly, a paraphrase may use literal language entirely but fail to capture an important aspect of meaning of the original text.

Examples of literal sentences:

  • I have to go to my country to see my father.
  • Turnbull busies himself with his clipboard as his reply is translated.
  • The diagrams are updated periodically during the course of the simulation.

Examples of non-literal sentences:

  • And tell me, sweet creature, do you count as a toy?
    This sentence addresses a person, describing the person as sweet and comparing the person to a toy. The basic meaning of sweet is to describe a sugary taste; its usage in this sentence thus compares the person to things that typically taste sweet, such as sugar. Similarly, as people are not toys, the comparison between the person and toys is non-literal. The usage of count is also non-literal, as it refers to an abstract thinking process of considering whether a person belongs to a particular category, whereas the word has a more concrete meaning of calculating the number of objects.
  • No longer can the Government disguise the crisis surrounding electricity privatisation.
    Both disguise and surround have a more concrete meaning than their meaning in this sentence.
  • I wouldn't want her to have a rough time.
    Rough refers to the texture of a surface, how the surface of something feels against your skin. Time apparently does not have a surface for us to touch; the usage is therefore non-literal.
